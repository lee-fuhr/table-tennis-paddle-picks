How to choose a ping pong paddles

Updated June 2026Independently researchedNo paid placement.

Quick answer

To choose a ping pong paddle, focus on your playing style (offensive, defensive, or all-round) and skill level, then pick a paddle construction (blade, sponge, rubber) that matches it. Pre-made paddles are fine for beginners, but advanced players should consider customizing. Grip shape and weight also affect control, so try before you buy if possible.

What grip shape should you choose?

The three most common grip shapes are flared, straight, and anatomic. Flared handles have a wider base that flares out at the end, helping you lock the paddle in your hand while reducing the chance of flying out during a big swing. Straight handles are uniform in width and suit players who like to shift their grip position frequently. Anatomic handles are contoured to fit the natural curve of your palm, which can feel more comfortable for some people. Your choice mostly comes down to hand size and personal feel. Many casual players prefer flared because it offers a secure hold without having to squeeze too hard. If you’re buying for a child or someone with small hands, a straight handle is often easier to control.

What paddle construction matters most?

A table tennis paddle is a sandwich of three layers: the blade (wood core, often with carbon or other fiber layers), the sponge (foam between blade and rubber), and the rubber sheet on top. The blade gives the paddle its stiffness and weight – a 5-ply all-wood blade is a safe starting point, while carbon or fiber blades make the paddle faster but less forgiving. The sponge thickness and hardness control the amount of energy the paddle returns. A thicker sponge (medium to thick range) gives more speed and spin potential, but demands good technique. A thinner sponge offers more control and feel. Rubber topsheets come in different textures: smooth (inverted) for generating spin with friction, or pimpled (short, long, anti-spin) for disrupting opponent’s spin or adding a different response. For most players, a smooth inverted rubber on both sides with a medium sponge is the most versatile choice.

How does your playing style affect paddle choice?

Offensive players need a paddle that can generate speed and spin. That typically means a stiffer blade (often with carbon), a harder and thicker sponge, and a tacky or grippy rubber topsheet. Defensive players, on the other hand, value control and the ability to return spin. They often choose a slower all-wood blade, thinner sponge, and sometimes long pimple rubber on one side to neutralize heavy spin. All-round players fall in the middle – they want balanced speed and control. A classic 5-ply all-wood blade with medium sponge and inverted rubber on both sides is the standard recommendation. If you’re unsure, start with an all-round paddle and adjust as your game develops. Many manufacturers label paddles as “offensive,” “defensive,” or “all-round” to simplify selection.

Should you buy a pre-made paddle or build your own?

Pre-made paddles are ready to use out of the box and are great for beginners and recreational players. Reputable brands like Stiga, Butterfly, Killerspin, and JOOLA offer packages that include a paddle, sometimes with a case and balls. These are usually consistent and affordable – you don’t need to worry about compatibility or assembly. Customizing lets you handpick the blade, sponge, and rubber for your exact preferences. It’s more expensive and requires some knowledge, but it gives you a paddle that truly fits your game. If you take the sport seriously and can practice often, a custom setup is the better long-term investment. However, even a mid-range pre-made paddle from a top brand will serve most people well for years.

What do speed and spin ratings really mean?

Many pre-made paddles advertise numbers like “speed 80, spin 90” or use stars (e.g., 4-star, 5-star). These ratings are not standardized across brands, so they should only be used as a rough guide. A 4-star paddle from one brand may feel quite different from another brand’s 4-star paddle. The numbers generally reflect the manufacturer’s own scale – higher numbers mean faster or spinnier potential, but not necessarily better for you. A better approach is to look at the actual construction: what blade wood, sponge thickness, and rubber type they use. Reading owner reviews and expert tests gives a more reliable picture. For beginners, a paddle rated “slow” or “control-oriented” will help you develop a feel for the ball before you upgrade to a faster setup.

How much should you spend for quality?

Entry-level paddles cost roughly the price of a fast-food meal for two, and can be fine for casual basement play. Moving into the budget-friendly range – roughly a few tens of dollars more – you start getting paddles made from real wood (not particle board) with sponge and decent rubber. At around a few hundred dollars, you enter the territory of high-quality pre-made paddles from top brands. Custom setups range widely depending on the components, but you can assemble a very competitive paddle for a couple hundred dollars. The key is to match your spending to your commitment. If you play once a month, a budget-friendly pre-made paddle is plenty. If you’re in a league or playing multiple times a week, it’s worth investing more. Remember that a paddle that’s too fast can make learning harder, so don’t equate price with suitability.

Frequently asked questions

What’s the difference between shakehand and penhold grips?

Shakehand grips involve holding the handle as if you’re shaking someone’s hand – your palm wraps around the handle and your index finger extends along the backhand side. Penhold grips mimic holding a pen, with the handle resting between your thumb and index finger and your other fingers curled on the back. Shakehand is more common worldwide and generally easier for beginners because it balances forehand and backhand power. Penhold can offer superior forehand reach and wrist flexibility but requires more work for backhand strokes.

Are all paddles ITTF approved?

No. The International Table Tennis Federation (ITTF) maintains a list of approved rubbers and blades for official competition. Pre-made paddles sold as “ITTF approved” use rubbers on that list. For casual play, you don’t need approval, but if you plan to enter tournaments, check the rubber’s ITTF logo on the topsheet. Some budget paddles may have a layer of glue that dries with a glossy surface, making them illegal in sanctioned events.

What sponge thickness should a beginner choose?

Beginners generally benefit from a thinner sponge – roughly the thickness of two stacked quarters. This gives more control and feel, making it easier to learn basic shots without the ball flying off too fast. Thicker sponges (around the thickness of a stack of four or five quarters) offer more speed and spin but require cleaner technique to control.

Can I use my ping pong paddle for pickleball or other paddlesports?

No. Table tennis paddles have a different surface, weight distribution, and bounce properties than pickleball or paddle tennis paddles. Using a table tennis paddle for pickleball will not perform well and could damage the rubber. It’s best to use the correct equipment for each sport.

How should I clean and store my paddle?

After each session, wipe the rubber surfaces with a damp (not wet) cloth to remove dust and sweat. Let them air dry, then apply a rubber protector sheet (many paddles come with them). Store the paddle flat in a padded case, away from direct sunlight and extreme temperatures. Never leave it in a hot car – heat can warp the blade and degrade the rubber.

Do I need a different paddle for a hardbat (no sponge) style?

Hardbat paddles – which use a thin layer of rubber directly on wood without sponge – create a different feel and lower speed. If you’re curious about the retro style, you can buy a hardbat-specific paddle or modify an existing one by replacing the rubber. Most hardbat players use specialized paddles designed for that classic dead-ball feel.

In shortChoosing a ping pong paddle doesn’t have to be intimidating. Start by identifying whether you’re a casual player or an aspiring competitor, then pick a grip shape that feels natural and a construction that matches your style. A well-chosen mid-range pre-made paddle from a reputable brand is often the smartest first step. As your game grows, you can explore custom setups – but even then, the most important thing is to get out and play. A paddle that fits your hand and your level will make every rally more enjoyable.
